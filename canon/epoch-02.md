## Ancient Near East — carry-overs by definitive form (3)

1. **Epic of Gilgamesh (Standard Babylonian; Sîn-lēqi-unninni)** — c. 1100 BCE. The first great literary epic and the earliest sustained meditation on human mortality in world literature; what makes the Standard Babylonian version specifically canonical — rather than its earlier Sumerian precursors — is Sîn-lēqi-unninni's editorial achievement in integrating the flood narrative into the mortality quest, creating a unified arc in which Gilgamesh's failure to retrieve the plant of immortality from the sea floor is the final, devastating answer to the question the death of Enkidu first opened, and producing for the first time in the record a narrative whose structure is itself an argument about the human condition. *George (Penguin).*
2. **Enūma Eliš** — c. 1100 BCE. The Babylonian creation epic and Genesis 1's essential counter-text; the correspondences — watery chaos before creation, the divine wind, the separation of waters above and below, the creation of humanity for divine service — are too structurally precise to be incidental, and the political theology the text encodes alongside its cosmogony, in which Marduk's supremacy in the divine council legitimises Babylon's supremacy among cities, establishes the template for the fusion of creation myth and imperial theology that recurs throughout the ancient world. *Dalley.*
3. **Instruction of Amenemope** — c. 1100–1000 BCE. Egyptian wisdom literature at its most spiritually interior and the direct documented source of Proverbs 22–24; the textual relationship — specific phrases, the structural correspondence of the "thirty sayings," shared images — is among the most clearly established cases of literary transmission in biblical scholarship, meaning that Amenemope does not merely parallel Proverbs but partially constitutes it, making this the text that most concretely demonstrates the Hebrew Bible's deep embeddedness in the wider literary culture of the ancient Near East. *Lichtheim II.*

## Levantine / Aramaic (1)

4. **Words of Ahiqar** — comp. 7th–6th c. BCE; Elephantine ms. c. 450 BCE. **[C]** The sole surviving monument of ancient Aramaic literature and the earliest extant example of the frame-narrative wisdom form — a story constructed specifically to carry embedded proverbs — predating the *Pañcatantra* by nearly a millennium; its direct influence on the Book of Tobit demonstrates its circulation within Jewish literary culture, its proverbs are demonstrably echoed in biblical wisdom literature, and without it the international, cross-cultural character of ancient Near Eastern wisdom transmission — the same sayings moving between Assyrian, Aramaic, and Hebrew contexts — would be entirely invisible to the record. *Lindenberger.*

## Iranian (1)

5. **Gāthās of Zarathustra (with Yasna Haptaŋhāiti)** — c. 1000–600 BCE [window flag]. The founding documents of Zoroastrian theology and the origin point of ethical dualism in world religion; the cosmic opposition between Ahura Mazdā and Angra Mainyu — truth against the Lie, light against darkness, with each human soul required to choose — is the theological innovation that enters Jewish thought during the Babylonian exile and thereby shapes the eschatology, angelology, and concept of the adversary that Christianity and Islam inherit, making the Gāthās not merely a text but the originating document of a moral framework whose consequences for Western religious history are incalculable, and whose philosophically dense Avestan verse remains among the most difficult and rewarding religious poetry in any ancient language. *Humbach & Faiss; Insler.*

## Chinese (3)

6. **Yijing (core text)** — c. 1000–800 BCE. The most continuously interpreted text in history and a cosmological achievement without structural parallel; its sixty-four hexagrams constitute not a scripture but a generative system — a combinatorial framework of binary notation whose permutations model change, transformation, and the relationship between heaven, earth, and human action — applied without interruption to cosmology, ethics, politics, medicine, and strategy across three millennia of Chinese thought, and sufficiently self-contained as a formal structure that Leibniz recognised in it an anticipation of binary arithmetic, a cross-cultural resonance that no other ancient text can claim. *Rutt.*
7. **Shujing (New Text chapters)** — center c. 1000–700 BCE. The source document of the Mandate of Heaven — Tianming — the political theology that governs Chinese thought on legitimacy, dynastic succession, and the moral accountability of rulers for three thousand years; every subsequent Chinese political text that addresses why dynasties rise and fall, and on what grounds rebellion is justified or condemned, operates within the conceptual framework these chapters establish, making the Shujing not merely a record of early Zhou ideology but the founding document of Chinese political philosophy as such. *Legge.*
8. **Shijing** — c. 1000–600 BCE. The oldest substantial anthology of world lyric and the founding text of the Chinese poetic tradition; its 305 poems — moving across folk song, court ode, lament, and sacrificial hymn — preserve the only substantial record of popular voice from the early Zhou period, establish the generic categories and tonal conventions within which all subsequent Chinese poetry operates, and were judged by Confucius to constitute the indispensable foundation of moral and aesthetic education, a verdict that made the Shijing the lens through which literate Chinese culture read both feeling and conduct for two millennia. *Waley; Legge.*

## Indian (2)

9. **Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad** — c. 800–600 BCE. The founding document of Indian metaphysics and the earliest sustained philosophical prose in any tradition; Yājñavalkya's dialogues — with Maitreyī on the self as the ground of all love, with Gārgī on what the world is woven upon, culminating in the negative definition of Brahman as neti neti — constitute the first systematic attempt in world philosophy to think the absolute through reason rather than myth, and the identification of ātman with Brahman that emerges from them is the axial insight around which the entire subsequent history of Indian thought — Advaita, Viśiṣṭādvaita, Buddhist reaction, and beyond — organises itself. *Olivelle (Oxford).*
10. **Chāndogya Upaniṣad** — c. 800–600 BCE. The great companion document to the Bṛhadāraṇyaka and the text in which the tat tvam asi — that art thou — formula receives its most extended and pedagogically deliberate exposition; the nine-stage dialogue between Uddālaka Āruṇi and his son Śvetaketu, in which the identity of the innermost self with the ground of all being is demonstrated through a sequence of physical analogies — salt dissolved in water, the seed of a fig tree — establishes a philosophical method of moving from sensory evidence to metaphysical conclusion that is without precedent in the ancient world and that Śaṅkara will later take as the interpretive cornerstone of Advaita Vedānta. *Olivelle.*

## Hebrew (6)

11. **Psalms** — core c. 1000–500 BCE. The greatest sustained collection of religious lyric in the ancient world and the text that most completely maps the emotional and spiritual range available to the human being before God; the lament psalms alone — the largest single genre within the collection, in which anguish, accusation, and abandonment are addressed directly to the divine without resolution — constitute an unmatched body of honest religious speech, and their formal innovations in parallelism, imagery, and antiphon become the governing conventions of all subsequent Western sacred lyric, from Christian hymnody to the devotional poetry of Islam. *Alter (Norton).*
12. **Amos** — c. 760 BCE. The first writing prophet and the text in which the axial theological argument of the Hebrew prophetic tradition receives its inaugural statement: that ethical conduct toward the poor is the criterion of authentic religion, displacing sacrificial ritual entirely — "I hate, I despise your feasts" — a move of such consequence that it reshapes not only Israelite religion but the entire subsequent moral theology of the Abrahamic traditions, and whose introduction of the Day of the Lord as an eschatological category becomes one of the most generative concepts in biblical thought. *Alter.*
13. **Isaiah** — c. 740–540 BCE (First + Deutero). The Hebrew Bible's supreme literary and theological achievement taken as a canonical unit; First Isaiah's universalist vision — the nations streaming to Zion, swords beaten into ploughshares — founds biblical messianism, while the Servant Songs of Deutero-Isaiah, in which a figure of vicarious suffering redeems through affliction rather than triumph, constitute the most consequential chapters in the history of biblical interpretation, read by Christianity as prophetic self-disclosure and generating a tradition of exegesis that has not yet exhausted itself. *Alter.*
14. **Jeremiah** — c. 627–586 BCE. The founding text of religious interiority in Western literature; the Confessions — the passages in which Jeremiah argues with God, expresses anguish at his vocation, and curses the day of his birth — are the first sustained body of introspective religious writing in any tradition, recording the psychic cost of prophetic calling from the inside in a way that no earlier religious literature attempts, and the New Covenant passage (31:31–34), in which the law is to be written on the heart rather than on stone, is among the most theologically consequential in the entire Hebrew Bible, foundational for Pauline theology and Christian thought on grace. *Alter.*
15. **Ezekiel** — c. 593–571 BCE. The founding document of Jewish mysticism and of apocalyptic literature simultaneously; the chariot vision of chapters 1–3 — the four living creatures, the wheels within wheels, the firmament and the throne — is the text from which merkabah mysticism takes its name and around whose exegesis the entire tradition of Jewish esotericism, from Hekhalot literature through Kabbalah, organises itself, while the bizarre cosmic imagery and the resurrection vision of the Valley of Dry Bones flow directly into Daniel and Revelation, making Ezekiel the single most generative source of the visionary mode in subsequent Jewish and Christian literature. *Alter.*
16. **Lamentations** — c. 586 BCE. Five poems of catastrophe, four of them acrostics — each verse beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet — a formal constraint that is itself a theological argument: the attempt to impose the full order of the alphabet on the experience of total destruction, producing a text in which form and content are in permanent and productive tension; as the only biblical book written entirely in the voice of grief without narrative resolution or theological consolation — the final verse is a question, not an answer — it occupies a position in the canon that nothing else in it holds. *Alter; O'Connor.*

## Greek (6)

17. **Iliad, Homer** — c. 750–700 BCE. The foundational epic of Western literature and its greatest sustained meditation on the cost of the heroic code; what makes it irreducible is not its subject — war — but its refusal to celebrate it: Achilles' choice between long life and glory, Hector's knowledge that Troy will fall, and above all the scene in Book 24 in which Priam supplicates his son's killer and two enemies recognise each other's grief, constitute a moral intelligence operating within the heroic world that dismantles that world's values even as it renders them magnificent. *Lattimore; Fagles.*
18. **Odyssey, Homer** — c. 750–700 BCE. The *Iliad*'s necessary complement and the founding text of the literature of return and identity; where the *Iliad* asks what glory costs, the *Odyssey* asks what remains of a man after twenty years of war and wandering — and answers through objects and bodies rather than declarations, the great bow, the scar, the bed rooted in living wood, each a test of whether Odysseus is still who he was, making this the first sustained literary exploration of identity as something that can be lost and must be recovered rather than simply asserted. *Wilson; Lattimore.*
19. **Theogony, Hesiod** — c. 700 BCE. The first systematic Greek cosmogony and the text that gives Western literature its map of the divine order; its account of how Chaos, Gaia, and Eros precede the gods, how Cronus swallows his children and is overthrown by Zeus — structurally inheriting the Kumarbi Cycle's succession myth — is not merely mythology but an attempt to explain the nature of reality through divine genealogy, and Hesiod's self-presentation as a named poet called by the Muses on Helicon introduces the first explicit claim of individual poetic vocation in the Greek tradition. *Most; West.*
20. **Works and Days, Hesiod** — c. 700 BCE. The first substantial didactic poem in Western literature and the text in which the myths of Prometheus, Pandora, and the Five Ages receive their most complete early Greek statement; the doctrine of the Five Ages — Gold, Silver, Bronze, Heroes, Iron — introduces the concept of historical decline that European thought will recur to from Ovid to Vico, while the poem's sustained argument that labour is honourable and justice requires effort constitutes a moral counterpoint to the aristocratic heroic values of the Homeric epics, giving Hesiod a democratising tendency that no other archaic Greek text carries so explicitly. *Most; West.*
21. **Sappho (collected fragments)** — c. 630–570 BCE. The most intense lyric voice of the ancient world and the founder of the lyric of erotic interiority; Fragment 31 — the symptoms of desire mapped with clinical precision onto the watching body, jealousy and longing rendered as physical collapse — is the first surviving text in Western literature to make the interior experience of desire its explicit subject rather than its occasion, and the sapphic stanza she defined becomes one of the most durable metres in the tradition, adopted by Catullus and Horace and carrying her formal intelligence forward through two and a half millennia of European lyric. *Carson, If Not, Winter.*
22. **Heraclitus (collected fragments)** — c. 500 BCE. The first Western philosopher to make contradiction a feature of reality rather than a problem to be eliminated; his doctrine of the Logos — a rational principle governing a universe in perpetual flux — is taken up by the Stoics and enters the Gospel of John's prologue, while his paradoxes (the road up and down is one and the same; you cannot step into the same river twice) constitute the earliest sustained philosophical attempt to think through the unity of opposites, a mode of thought whose consequences run from Plato's dialectic through Hegel to contemporary philosophy of language, making the fragments among the most generative seventy sentences in the intellectual tradition. *Kahn (Cambridge).*
