## Standing Rulings

The argument above establishes why a canon of this kind should exist and on what philosophical grounds it rests. What follows establishes, with equal precision, how it was built. The rulings set out here are not post-hoc rationalisations of decisions already taken; they are the procedural commitments to which every entry, and every exclusion, is held. A reader who understands them understands not merely what the canon contains but why it is the shape that it is — and is therefore equipped to challenge any verdict within it on its own terms.

Every entry is placed at the date of its definitive form, and it enters in that form alone. The Old Babylonian *Gilgamesh* gives way to the Standard Babylonian recension of Sîn-lēqi-unninni; it is the latter that holds the canonical place. This is not a philological judgement about which version preserves the most authentic text — authenticity and achievement are distinct questions — but a recognition that the form which has generated the widest and most sustained engagement is, in almost every case, the form in which the work reached its fullest literary realisation. Distinct works within a genre lineage are not, however, treated as superseding one another: the *Pyramid Texts* and the *Book of the Dead* are separate achievements answering separate purposes, and each holds its own canonical entry. Supersession obtains only where a later work so completely absorbs the contribution of an earlier one that independent retention of both would be redundant.

Orally accreted corpora require a different handling, because they reached written form through processes of compilation and redaction that may span centuries and that are, in many cases, not fully recoverable. For such works — the *Ṛgveda*, the *Muʿallaqāt*, the *Heike*, the *Guru Granth Sāhib*, the *Kumulipo*, the *Dede Korkut* — the canon places the entry at the compositional centre: the period during which the work's core material was formed, rather than the date of its final redaction or its first written transcription. Where that centre is itself contested, the range is given in preference to a false precision. A related problem arises with works embedded within larger compilations but possessing fully independent textual or liturgical lives of their own. Such works — the *Bhagavad Gītā*, extracted from the *Mahābhārata*; the *Devī Māhātmya*; the *Daxue* and *Zhongyong*, elevated by Zhu Xi's commentary into independent canonical objects; the Kana Preface of the *Kokinshū*; the *Poruḷatikāram* — enter the canon as works in their own right. Inclusion under this principle is not editorial arbitrariness: it is a recognition that the boundaries of a work are determined by the history of its reception as much as by the circumstances of its original composition.

The question of redundancy governs a significant proportion of the canon's exclusions. A work is cut when its contribution is substantially carried by another entry — through supersession, absorption, or an overlap so extensive that the independent inclusion of both would yield nothing a serious reader could not already obtain from one. A distinct argument, a distinct influence, or a distinct formal achievement defeats redundancy: uniqueness along any one of these dimensions is sufficient for retention. Redundancy is among the most consequential rulings in the canon's construction, and it is also the ruling most susceptible to legitimate challenge, because the judgement of whether one work's contribution is genuinely subsumed by another is never entirely mechanical; every such determination is documented so that it can be disputed.

Inscribed, legal, and political documents qualify for inclusion when they constitute intellectual achievements in their own right — when they are not merely functional instruments but works whose argumentation, ethical ambition, or literary reach place them in the company of the canon's other entries. The Code of Hammurabi, the Major Rock Edicts of Aśoka, the *Corpus Iuris Civilis*, the Declaration of Independence, and the Letter from Birmingham Jail enter on this basis. The principle is not that every historically significant document belongs — the canon is not an archive of consequence — but that the document-as-work is a legitimate literary and intellectual form and one that, at its peaks, is fully canonical. Similarly, the lone surviving vehicle of an entire intellectual position or literary mode qualifies for inclusion regardless of the accidents of its transmission. The *Tattvopaplavasiṃha* of Jayarāśi survives as the only extant text of Indian radical scepticism. The Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus is the sole vehicle of Bronze Age clinical rationalism. Marguerite Porete's *Mirror of Simple Souls* was burned with its author and remained unidentified for six centuries. In each such case, the work holds its canonical place not merely because it is unique but because its uniqueness corresponds to a position — an argument, a mode, a tradition — that the record would otherwise contain no trace of. A canon that excluded works on the grounds of their fragile or belated transmission would be a canon of what survived conveniently, not of what was achieved.

A further ruling governs an important distinction. Purely technical monuments — works that function primarily as instruments of specialist practice rather than as continuous reading experiences available to the intellectually ambitious non-specialist — fail the criterion of inclusion regardless of their importance to the history of their disciplines. Gaṅgeśa's *Tattvacintāmaṇi*, Ockham's *Summa Logicae*, the mathematical works of Gauss, and the *Principia Mathematica* of Russell and Whitehead are achievements of the first order within their fields; they are not works in the relevant sense. The distinction at stake here is not difficulty — the *Critique of Pure Reason* is formidably difficult, and it passes — but the nature of the barrier to entry. A difficult work repays the sustained effort of a serious general reader; a purely technical work is accessible only to those with prior specialist formation, and no amount of sustained effort by the general reader alters that. Frege's *Grundlagen der Arithmetik* passes this test; the *Grundgesetze der Arithmetik* does not.

Certain classes of work are excluded structurally, not on a case-by-case basis but as a matter of category. Chronicles, as a class, fall below the threshold at which historical recording becomes historical and literary achievement of the first rank. Travel accounts are similarly excluded — with one principled exception: Evliya Çelebi's *Seyahatnâme*, whose literary artistry and synthetic range place it in a category that the genre it formally inhabits cannot adequately describe. Conduct books, reference monuments, works whose content consists primarily of systematic compilation without interpretive or argumentative development, and oral epics that have not reached a definitive textual form are excluded on the same grounds. Every such exclusion is documented rather than silenced. The canon's omissions are part of its record, and they are held to the same standard of honesty as its inclusions.

Finally, the criteria of durability and resilience under scrutiny cannot be applied with confidence to works produced within living memory, because the evidence those criteria require — recognition across substantially different historical contexts, survival under sustained critical challenge — has not yet had time to accumulate. Full canonical confidence is therefore extended only to works completed before 1975. Works from the period 1975 to 2000 are admitted provisionally, marked **[P]**, where early scholarly consensus is sufficiently broad and convergent to constitute a credible anticipation of longer-term canonical standing; these entries are explicitly revisable as the evidence matures. Works completed after 2000 are registered on a watchlist only. The bar cannot yet be applied in good conscience, and the pretence that it could be would corrupt the methodology on which the rest of the canon depends.

### Notation used throughout this canon

- **[C]** — contestable inclusion: the work holds a place in the canon, but a reasonable case for its exclusion can be made and is documented.
- **[C+]** — strongly contestable exclusion: the work does not hold a place in the canon proper, but the case for its inclusion is sufficiently strong to constitute a standing challenge.
- **[P]** — provisional: admitted on the strength of early scholarly consensus; explicitly subject to revision.
- *Italics* — recommended translation or edition.

Dating, attribution, and transmission flags are preserved in the entry annotations wherever they bear materially on the evidential status of the work.
